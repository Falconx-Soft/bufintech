    Table 4.2: Options Greeks

    Greeks Description
    Delta
    • Delta is the change in an option's price in response to a change in the underlying's price.
    • Delta for long calls is always positive 0 < δcall < 1 and δATMCall ≈ 0.5
    • Delta for long puts is always negative -1 < δput < 0 and δATMCall ≈ -0.5
    • δcall = 1 + δput for options on the same underlying with the same BSM inputs (Put-Call Parity).
    • For non-dividend paying stocks, the delta of a forward contract is 1
    Gamma
    • Gamma is the change in an option's delta for a change in the underlying's price (a measure of curvature).
    • Gamma for long calls and long puts is always positive.
    • Gamma is highest for an option that is closest to ATM and near expiration, i.e. for which the delta hedge is most difficult to maintain.
    Vega
    • Vega is the change in an option's price for a change in the underlying's price volatility.
    • Vega for long calls and long puts is always positive.
    Theta
    • Theta is the daily change in the option's price, measuring the sensitivity of the option's price to the passage of time, known as time decay.
    • Theta for long calls and long puts is generally negative.

    Table 4.12: Volatility

    Implied Volatility
    • Implied volatility is the unobservable volatility that equates an option pricing model, e.g. Black–Scholes–Merton (BSM) model, to the market price given other variables.
    Realized Volatility
    • Square root of the variance of returns δa = δm √(252/21).
    Volatility Smile
    • U-shaped symmetric curve.
    Volatility Skew
    • Asymmetric version of the smile because investors geenerally have less interest in OTM calls in a bullish market but have more interest in OTM put options in a bearish market to protect against a market sell-off.
    Term Structure of Volatility
    • Options with the same strike price but different expirations display different implied volatilities.
    Volatility Surface
    • A 3D plot for put and call options on the same underlying asset where implied volatilties on the z-axis, days to expiration on the x-axis, and strike prices on the y-axis.
    VIX
    • VIX is a 30 day implied volatility calculated based on ATM and OTM S&P options over a wide range of strikes with weekly and standard options.

    Table 12.10: Performance Appraisal - Risk Based Measures

    Sharpe Ratio Sharpe Ratio: SA = [RA-rf]/ σA
    • Excess return above the risk-free rate above the risk-free rate per unit of return volatility.
    • Use of standard deviation as a risk measure comes with drawbacks:
      • standard deviation does not differentiate between upside and downside risk
      • using infrequent observations can underestimate the risk, overestimate the Sharpe Ratio
      • σA corresponds to total risk, including the volatility of both market and the alpha
    Treynor Ratio Treynor Ratio: TA = [RA-rf]/ σA
    • Excess return above the risk-free rate above the risk-free rate per unit of systematic risk.
    • For negative values of beta, the ratio maybe less intuitive.
    • Reliance on beta as a risk measure instead of the portfolio's own volatility requires comparisons with the same benchmark index.
    Information Ratio Information Ratio: IR=(E[rP]-E[rf])/ σ[rP - rf]
    • Excess return above a corresponding benchmark per unit of relative risk.
    • The usage of information ratio requires the choice a suitable benchmark for the investment strategy.
    • The IR is calculated by dividing the portfolio's mean excess return relative to the tracking error.
    Appraisal Ratio Appraisal Ratio: AR = σ/ σE
    • Annualized alpha divided by the annualized residual risk, i.e. portfolio's unsystematic risk.
    • Both the alpha and the residual risk are computed from a factor regression.
    • α =rP - [rf + β . (rM - rf)], the return associated to security selection
    • σE = Total Risk - Systematic Risk = UnSystematic Risk
    Sortino Ratio Sortino Ratio: SRD = [rP - rT] / σD , where rs = target rate of return = MAR = minimum acceptable return
    • Excess return above a MAR per unit of the volatility of returns < MAR, penalizing only the returns that are lower than a target return.
    • rT is set based on investor-specific preferences, rather than market conditions.
    • σD = ΣNi=1 min(rt - rT, 0)2/N
    • Choice of Sortino ratio over Sharpe ratio could be due to the fact that: (i) portfolio includes options (ii) return distributions are asymmetric (iii) investor's primary objective is capital preservation...
    Capture Ratios Capture Ratio (CR): UCR(m,t)/DCR(m,t), measuring the manager's relative performance in up & down markets.
    • UCR: UCR(m, t) = R(m, t)/R(B,t) if R(B,t) > 0
      • The upside capture ratio (UCR) measures capture when the benchmark return is positive.
      • UCR > 1 suggests outperformance relative to the benchmark
    • DCR: DCR(m, t) = R(m, t)/R(B, t) if R(B,t) < 0
      • The downside capture ratio (DCR) measures capture when the benchmark is negative.
      • DCR < 1 suggests outperformance relative to the benchmark
    • To calculate the UCR or DCR, calculate the simple or geometric average of returns for both the portfolio and benchmark separately that meet the condition & then take the ratio.
      • CR > 1: positive asymmetry & convex return profile
      • CR < 1: negative asymmetry & concave return profile

    • Drawdown is the cumulative peak-to-trough loss during a continuous period. The smaller the better.
    • Drawdown duration is the total time from the start of the drawdown until the cumulative drawdown recovers to zero. It has two parts:
      • drawdown phase (start to trough): The shorter the drawdown duration, the less time is required for the portfolio to recover from losses. Lower drawdown duration would be suitable for short time horizon
      • recovery phase (trough-to-zero cumulative return)
